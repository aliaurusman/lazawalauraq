Caring for Second-Hand Books

Every book on our shelves has already lived one life. A little care is what lets it live a second, and a third. Whether you have just bought a pre-loved book from us or you are clearing space to sell your own, here is how we keep old books healthy — no special tools, nothing you cannot find at home.

Clean gently, never wetly

Start with a dry, soft cloth or a clean makeup brush to lift dust from the cover and the top edge of the pages, where it gathers most. For a sticky mark on a hardcover, a barely-damp cloth wiped quickly and dried at once is safe — but keep water away from paper itself, because damp is what warps pages and invites mould. If a book smells musty, stand it open in fresh air and indirect light for a day rather than spraying anything on it.

Fix small damage before it grows

A loose page is not a ruined book. Slide a thin line of acid-free glue along its inner edge and press it back into the spine under a heavy weight overnight. Reinforce a tearing paperback spine with proper bookbinding tape rather than ordinary sticky tape, which yellows and stains within a year. And resist the urge to "flatten" a dog-eared corner too hard — a gentle fold-back is kinder to old paper than a sharp crease in the other direction.

Store them so they last

Shelve books upright, spine out, packed snugly enough to stand without leaning but loosely enough to pull one free without tugging. Keep them out of direct sunlight, which fades covers, and away from damp outer walls and bathrooms. Very large or heavy volumes are happier lying flat. And give them a little breathing room from the wall so air can move — trapped stillness is what old paper likes least.

On the marks a book already carries

A previous reader's pencil note, a pressed bus ticket, a name inked on the first page — we never think of these as damage. They are the proof that a book was loved before it reached you, and part of why a second-hand book is worth choosing over a shrink-wrapped new one. Clean a book, yes; repair it, gladly. But you do not have to erase its history to give it a future.

Look after a book this way and it stays ready for its next chapter — which, if you ever decide to pass it on, is exactly where Lazawal Auraq comes in.
